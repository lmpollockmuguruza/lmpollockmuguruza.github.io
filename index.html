Working Papers

"Corruption, Social Networks, and School Cheating" — MSc PSPE Dissertation (Department of Government, LSE, 2024-25)

Abstract: Do social networks moderate the effects of corruption on exam cheating? How do rural localities differ from urban localities in their response to corruption revelations? Using a novel dataset with locality-level family network measures for rural Mexico, I provide a compelling answer to these two questions, with additional data from Mexico's municipal audits and secondary school exam cheating rates. Using a staggered difference-in-differences identification strategy with modern estimators, I find that family networks moderate the effect of corruption revelations on exam cheating rates, with a positive, non-monotonic relationship between network strength and effect size. The analysis reveals that average degree (local connectivity) drives stronger effects than largest eigenvalue (global centrality), providing suggestive evidence that peer coordination rather than information cascades explains network moderation.

In time, those Unconscionable Maps no longer satisfied, and the Cartographers Guilds struck a Map of the Empire whose size was that of the Empire, and which coincided point for point with it. The following Generations, who were not so fond of the Study of Cartography as their Forebears had been, saw that that vast Map was Useless, and not without some Pitilessness was it, that they delivered it up to the Inclemencies of Sun and Winters.
